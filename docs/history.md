From its origins in 19th-century China to its global presence as a competitive sport and beloved pastime, Mahjong has a rich and fascinating history spanning over 150 years.

## Origins

### The Confucius Myth

A persistent legend attributes Mahjong's creation to Confucius around 500 BCE. According to this tale, Confucius was fond of birds — which supposedly explains why the game is called "Mahjong" (literally "sparrow" in some dialects). The myth claims that the three dragon tiles represent the three cardinal virtues Confucius promoted: benevolence (Red), sincerity (Green), and filial piety (White).

While this is a charming story, it is almost certainly false. There is no historical evidence linking Confucius to Mahjong, and the game in its recognizable form does not appear in any records until the mid-to-late 1800s. The myth was likely popularized in the early 20th century by Western writers seeking an exotic origin story.

### The Reality: 19th Century China

The most widely accepted historical account places Mahjong's origins in the Yangtze River Delta region during the 1860s-1870s. The game likely evolved from earlier Chinese card games, particularly:

- **Madiao (Horse Hanging)**: A card game from the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644) that used 40 cards with similar suit structures.
- **Yezi (Leaf Game)**: Another historical card game with some structural similarities.
- **Domino games**: Chinese domino traditions influenced the move from cards to tiles.

The transition from cards to tiles was a natural evolution. Tiles were more durable, easier to shuffle, and could stand upright on the table — allowing players to view their hand without holding anything.

### Who Invented It?

No single inventor can be conclusively identified. Several theories exist:

- **Chen Yumen**: A resident of Ningbo who allegedly created the game around 1870. Some Chinese sources credit him, though hard evidence is scarce.
- **Military officers**: Some accounts suggest the game was developed by Chinese military officials during the Taiping Rebellion (1850-1864) as a way to pass time.
- **Fishermen of the Yangtze**: Another theory suggests that fishermen adapted existing card games into a tile format.

The truth is likely that Mahjong evolved organically from pre-existing games, with many contributors refining the rules over decades. By the 1890s, the game had spread throughout China and was recognizable as the Mahjong we know today.

### Early Tile Design

The earliest Mahjong sets were crafted from bamboo and bone. The tiles were typically made with a bamboo back and a bone or ivory face, into which the designs were carved and painted. Higher-quality sets used cattle bone or even true ivory for the faces.

The three suits were established early:
- **Tong (Circles)**: Representing copper coins (tong qian), circular with square holes.
- **Tiao (Bamboo)**: Representing strings of 100 coins, traditionally tied with string.
- **Wan (Characters)**: Representing ten-thousand coin units, marked with Chinese numerals.

This monetary theme reflected the game's connection to earlier gambling card games where the suits literally represented units of currency.

## Evolution of the Game

### Standardization Challenges

In its early decades, Mahjong had no standardized rules. Each region, each household, and sometimes each game had its own variations. Common areas of disagreement included:

- The number of tiles (ranging from 120 to 160+ in various versions)
- Whether flower and season tiles were included
- Scoring systems (widely variable)
- Which combinations counted as valid winning hands
- Rules for claiming discards

### The Role of Flowers and Seasons

The eight bonus tiles (four flowers and four seasons) were added to the game relatively early, likely in the late 19th century. Their inclusion was not universal:

- Southern Chinese variants embraced flowers enthusiastically, making them central to scoring.
- Northern Chinese variants tended to minimize or exclude them.
- When the game moved to Japan, flowers were eventually dropped entirely from the standard ruleset.

### The Gambling Connection

From its earliest days, Mahjong was closely associated with gambling. The game's combination of skill and luck made it ideal for wagering, and Mahjong parlors became common throughout China. This connection had lasting consequences:

- The Chinese government has periodically attempted to regulate or suppress Mahjong gambling.
- During the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), Mahjong was banned in China as a symbol of bourgeois decadence and gambling vice.
- The ban was lifted in the late 1970s during the reform era, and Mahjong quickly regained its popularity.

### Early Scoring Systems

The original Chinese scoring systems were relatively simple compared to modern Riichi Mahjong. The basic concept was:

- Certain hand compositions were recognized as special and awarded bonus points.
- The system was additive: each bonus pattern added a fixed number of points.
- "Limit hands" (maximum-scoring hands) were recognized but rare.

Over time, different regions developed increasingly complex and divergent scoring systems, contributing to the proliferation of variants we see today.

## Global Spread

### Joseph Park Babcock and the Western Introduction

The man most responsible for bringing Mahjong to the Western world was **Joseph Park Babcock**, an American businessman working for Standard Oil in Soochow (Suzhou), China, in the early 1910s. Babcock observed the game's popularity in China and saw a business opportunity.

In 1920, Babcock wrote a simplified rulebook he titled "Rules of Mah-Jongg" (he trademarked this specific spelling). He added Arabic numerals and index letters to the tiles to make them readable for Westerners who could not read Chinese characters. He also simplified the rules considerably, removing many of the regional complications.

Babcock's genius was in marketing. He partnered with the import firm W.A. Hammond to sell Mahjong sets in the United States, and the game exploded in popularity.

### The 1920s Mahjong Craze

The American Mahjong craze of the 1920s was extraordinary in its scale and intensity:

- **1922-1924**: Mahjong became the most popular parlor game in America, surpassing bridge temporarily.
- By 1923, Mahjong sets were outselling radios in the United States.
- Over 1.5 million sets were sold in the U.S. between 1922 and 1924.
- The demand was so high that cattle bone supplies ran short, and manufacturers experimented with alternatives including Bakelite (an early plastic).
- Mahjong-themed songs, clothing, and accessories became fashionable.
- Eddie Cantor performed a popular song called "Since Ma Is Playing Mah Jong."
- Abercrombie & Fitch became one of the largest Mahjong set retailers in the country.

The craze extended beyond America. Mahjong became popular across Europe, particularly in Britain, France, and the Netherlands. In each country, the rules were adapted to local preferences, further diversifying the game.

### The Inevitable Decline

By the mid-1920s, the American Mahjong craze was already fading. Several factors contributed:

- The lack of standardized rules caused confusion and frustration.
- Multiple competing rulebooks created factions of players who could not agree on how to play.
- The game's initial novelty wore off.
- Bridge, with its highly standardized rules and organized tournament structure, reasserted its dominance as the premier Western card/parlor game.

However, Mahjong never disappeared entirely from the West. It persisted in immigrant communities and among dedicated enthusiasts, eventually finding a permanent niche through the National Mah Jongg League (see American Mahjong below).

### The National Mah Jongg League

In 1937, the **National Mah Jongg League (NMJL)** was founded in New York City, primarily serving Jewish-American women players. The NMJL brought much-needed standardization to American Mahjong by:

- Publishing an annual card of approved winning hands.
- Establishing consistent tournament rules.
- Creating a community and organizational structure.

The NMJL remains active today and continues to publish its annual card, making American Mahjong uniquely dynamic — the valid winning hands change every year.

## Regional Variants

### How Variants Diverged

As Mahjong spread across Asia and around the world, regional variants developed based on local preferences, cultural factors, and the specific rules that were transmitted to each area. The major variants that exist today are the product of over a century of independent evolution.

### Chinese Official (Guobiao Mahjong)

In 1998, the Chinese State Sports Commission published an official set of Mahjong rules called **Guobiao Mahjong** (National Standard Mahjong). This was an attempt to create a unified Chinese ruleset for competitive play.

Key features:
- 81 recognized winning patterns (fan), organized by point value
- Uses flower and season tiles
- Minimum of 8 points to declare a win
- Emphasis on complex, high-scoring patterns
- Intended for tournament play

While Guobiao exists as an official standard, casual Chinese Mahjong players often continue to use regional house rules that may differ significantly from the national standard.

### Hong Kong Mahjong

Hong Kong Mahjong (also called Cantonese Mahjong) is one of the most widely played variants globally, popular throughout Hong Kong, Guangdong province, and overseas Cantonese communities.

Key features:
- Uses 144 tiles (including flowers and seasons)
- Scoring in "faan" (fan) with a typical minimum of 3 faan
- Relatively simple scoring compared to Riichi or Guobiao
- No riichi or furiten rules
- "Chicken hand" (0 faan) is sometimes allowed in casual play
- Fast-paced games

### Taiwanese Mahjong

Taiwanese Mahjong is distinctly different from other variants:

Key features:
- **16-tile hand** (instead of the standard 13)
- 144 tiles with heavy emphasis on flower tiles
- Flowers are a major scoring component, not just bonus tiles
- Unique "tai" scoring system
- More aggressive play style due to the 16-tile hand offering more flexibility
- Popular in Taiwan and among Taiwanese diaspora communities

### Sichuan Mahjong (Bloody Rules)

Sichuan Mahjong, popular in China's Sichuan province, has some of the most distinctive rules of any variant:

Key features:
- Only uses the three suited tiles (no honors or flowers) — 108 tiles total
- "Bloody" rules: when one player wins, the game continues until three players have won or the wall is exhausted
- Each player must eliminate one suit from their hand by the time they win
- Fast, aggressive, and popular in Chinese mahjong apps

### Korean Mahjong

Korean Mahjong has its own twist:

Key features:
- Similar base to Japanese Mahjong but with notable differences
- Does not use the furiten rule
- Has a unique "flower" system using number tiles
- Less popular than other variants but has a dedicated following

## Japanese Riichi Mahjong

### Post-War Development

Mahjong arrived in Japan in the early 1900s, but it was after World War II that the game underwent its most significant Japanese transformation. During the post-war period, several key innovations emerged:

- **Riichi (Reach) declaration**: The signature mechanic that gives the variant its name. The riichi rule was likely introduced in the 1920s-1930s but became standard in the post-war era. It added a strategic layer of risk-reward by allowing players to "bet" on their hand being one tile away from completion.

- **Furiten rule**: The restriction preventing players from winning on tiles they have already discarded. This rule was developed to eliminate certain contradictory and unfair situations, and it became a cornerstone of Japanese Mahjong strategy.

- **Dora system**: The dora indicator tiles that provide bonus scoring. This system was added to inject more variance and excitement into the game. The red five (akadora) variant was a later addition, appearing around the 1960s.

### Mahjong Parlors (Jansou)

Mahjong parlors (jansou) became a staple of Japanese urban life in the post-war decades. At their peak:

- There were over 40,000 mahjong parlors across Japan.
- They were common in entertainment districts, near train stations, and on university campuses.
- Parlors served as social hubs where salarymen, students, and retirees would gather.
- Many parlors operated as de facto gambling establishments, despite gambling being technically illegal in Japan.
- The parlor culture significantly influenced the development of Riichi Mahjong rules, as parlors needed standardized rules for their customers.

The number of parlors has declined since their 1980s peak (down to approximately 8,000-10,000 as of the 2020s), partly due to demographic changes and the rise of online Mahjong.

### Professional Leagues

Japan has several professional Mahjong organizations:

- **Japan Professional Mahjong League (JPML / Nihon Pro Maajan Renmei)**: Founded in 1981, the oldest and most prestigious. Known for its conservative rules (no red fives, no open tanyao).
- **Professional Mahjong Organization (PML / Saikyousen)**: Another major league with a different rule emphasis.
- **M-League**: Founded in 2018, a team-based professional league with corporate sponsorship (teams owned by companies like Konami, Sega, and Dentsu). M-League brought Mahjong into the mainstream sports broadcasting sphere with televised matches and high production values.
- **Mu-League, RMU, and others**: Additional professional organizations with varying rules and prestige.

Professional players in Japan can earn significant income through tournament prizes, sponsorships, and media appearances, though only the top tier rivals athletes in traditional sports.

### Tenhou and Online Riichi

**Tenhou** (Tenhou.net) was launched in 2006 and became the gold standard for competitive online Riichi Mahjong. Its influence on the development of Riichi Mahjong cannot be overstated:

- Tenhou's ranking system (from beginner to Tenhoui, the highest rank) became a benchmark for player skill.
- The platform's data enabled statistical analysis of Mahjong strategy for the first time at scale.
- Tenhou popularized the "red five" rules that are now standard in most casual Riichi play.
- The platform's ladder system encouraged competitive play and provided a clear path for skill improvement.

Achieving the rank of "Tenhoui" on Tenhou is considered one of the most prestigious accomplishments in the Mahjong world, requiring sustained excellent play over thousands of games.

## Competitive Mahjong

### World Championships

Several organizations host international Mahjong tournaments:

- **World Mahjong Championship (WMC)**: Organized by the World Mahjong Organization, using Guobiao (Chinese Official) rules. First held in 2007 in Chengdu, China.
- **World Riichi Championship (WRC)**: Organized by the European Mahjong Association, using Riichi rules. Held biennially since 2014.
- **Mahjong International League (MIL)**: Hosts various international competitions.
- **World Series of Mahjong**: A Hong Kong-based tournament series with significant prize pools.

### The Rise of Western Competitive Riichi

Starting in the 2000s, Riichi Mahjong gained a significant following outside Asia:

- The **European Mahjong Association (EMA)** was founded in 2005 and oversees competitive Mahjong across Europe.
- National Mahjong associations formed in France, Germany, the Netherlands, the UK, and many other European countries.
- The EMA's ranking system tracks tournament results across Europe.
- North American organizations like **USPML (United States Professional Mahjong League)** emerged to organize competitive play.

This growth was driven by several factors: anime and manga featuring Mahjong, the accessibility of online platforms, and the globalization of Japanese pop culture.

### Online Competitive Platforms

## Cultural Significance

### Mahjong in Chinese Culture

Mahjong holds a unique place in Chinese culture that goes far beyond being "just a game":

- **Family bonding**: In many Chinese families, Mahjong is the default social activity during holidays, especially Chinese New Year. Multi-generational games — grandparents playing with grandchildren — are common and treasured.
- **Social currency**: Knowing how to play Mahjong is considered a basic social skill in many Chinese communities. Not being able to play can be a social disadvantage.
- **Business relationships**: Mahjong games have traditionally served as venues for building business relationships (guanxi). The informal setting allows for relationship building that formal meetings cannot provide.
- **Retirement activity**: Mahjong parlors and community centers serve as vital social hubs for elderly Chinese, combating isolation and providing mental stimulation.

### The Sound of the Tiles

The distinctive sound of Mahjong tiles being shuffled (called "washing" the tiles) is deeply evocative for many Chinese and Japanese people. The clicking and clattering of tiles is often described as:

- A sound of home and family gatherings
- The "music" of Chinese New Year
- A nostalgic connection to childhood memories

Automatic Mahjong tables, which shuffle tiles mechanically beneath the playing surface, have become common in homes and parlors. While convenient, some traditionalists feel they lack the tactile pleasure and familiar sound of manual shuffling.

### Superstitions and Customs

Mahjong has accumulated a rich layer of superstitions over the decades:

- **Seat selection**: Many players believe certain seats are luckier than others and will perform rituals to secure a good seat.
- **Wearing red**: Some players wear red undergarments for luck, particularly during Chinese New Year games.
- **Tile touching**: Some players will not touch tiles with their left hand, or will tap the table before drawing.
- **Book avoidance**: The word for "book" (shu) sounds like "lose" in some Chinese dialects, so some players avoid saying or handling books near the Mahjong table.
- **No tapping on shoulders**: Tapping a player's shoulder during a game is considered extremely bad luck.
- **Ghost month**: Some players avoid Mahjong during the seventh month of the lunar calendar (Ghost Month), believing that spirits may influence the game.

### Mahjong and Gender

Mahjong's relationship with gender varies by culture:

- In China, Mahjong is played by all genders, but stereotypically associated with middle-aged and elderly women in everyday social contexts (while competitive and gambling contexts skew male).
- In Japan, Mahjong parlors have traditionally been male-dominated spaces, though this is changing with the rise of online play and anime-influenced media.
- In America, the NMJL tradition is strongly associated with Jewish-American women, representing a distinctive female social space maintained for nearly a century.

## Mahjong in Media

### Film

Mahjong has featured in numerous films across Asian cinema:

- **"Kung Fu Mahjong" trilogy (2005-2007)**: Hong Kong comedy films centered on Mahjong gambling, starring Wong Jing.
- **"God of Gamblers" (1989)**: While primarily about poker, this iconic Hong Kong film starring Chow Yun-fat includes notable Mahjong scenes.
- **"Crazy Rich Asians" (2018)**: Features a pivotal Mahjong scene between the protagonist Rachel Chu and her boyfriend's mother Eleanor Young. The scene uses Mahjong as a metaphor for their cultural and interpersonal conflict, bringing the game to mainstream Western cinema audiences.
- **"The Joy Luck Club" (1993)**: Opens with a Mahjong game that frames the narrative, connecting the immigrant experience with Chinese cultural traditions.
- **"Lust, Caution" (2007)**: Ang Lee's film features prominent Mahjong scenes set in 1940s Shanghai, where the game serves as a cover for espionage.

### Anime and Manga

Mahjong has a substantial presence in Japanese anime and manga:

- **"Akagi" (1991-2018, manga; 2005-2006, anime)**: Perhaps the most famous Mahjong manga, written by Nobuyuki Fukumoto. It tells the story of Shigeru Akagi, a genius gambler who dominates the underground Mahjong world. The series is renowned for its psychological intensity and dramatic portrayal of Mahjong as a battle of wits.

- **"Kaiji" (1996-present, manga; 2007-2011, anime)**: Also by Fukumoto, features Mahjong arcs among its various gambling scenarios. Known for its themes of desperation, debt, and human nature under pressure.

- **"Saki" (2006-present, manga; 2009, anime)**: A more lighthearted take, featuring high school girls competing in Mahjong tournaments. Saki is notable for introducing Mahjong to a younger, broader audience and for its distinctive blend of cute character designs with intense Mahjong gameplay. The series has been credited with increasing female interest in Riichi Mahjong.

- **"The Legend of Koizumi" (2006-2015)**: A satirical manga where world leaders resolve international conflicts through Mahjong. Featuring outrageous depictions of real political figures playing supernatural Mahjong, it is equal parts absurd comedy and genuine Mahjong education.

- **"Mudazumo Naki Kaikaku"**: Features world leaders battling through Mahjong, blending political satire with over-the-top Mahjong action.

- **"Tetsuya" (1997-2005)**: Set in post-WWII Japan, following a Mahjong hustler in the streets of Shinjuku. Known for its historical setting and realistic portrayal of the Japanese Mahjong underworld.

### Video Games

Mahjong has been a staple of video gaming since the early days:

- **Yakuza (Like a Dragon) series**: Sega's popular action-adventure franchise has featured Mahjong as a mini-game since its first installment in 2005. For many Western gamers, the Yakuza series was their first exposure to Riichi Mahjong. The in-game Mahjong is surprisingly authentic and complete.

- **Clubhouse Games (51 Worldwide Classics)**: Nintendo's 2020 Switch game includes a complete Riichi Mahjong implementation, introducing the game to Nintendo's massive audience.

- **Final Fantasy XIV**: Square Enix added a full Riichi Mahjong mode (Doman Mahjong) to their massively popular MMORPG in 2019, exposing millions of players to the game.

- **Classic arcade Mahjong**: Japanese arcades have featured Mahjong video games since the 1980s, often with adult-themed "strip Mahjong" variants. More legitimate competitive arcade Mahjong games like Sega MJ have maintained a following.

## Digital Mahjong

### Early Computer Implementations

Computer Mahjong has a long history:

- The earliest known computer Mahjong programs appeared in the late 1970s and early 1980s on Japanese personal computers.
- The 1981 game "Shanghai" (later known as "Mahjong Solitaire") created a single-player tile-matching game using Mahjong tiles. Despite having nothing to do with actual Mahjong, this game became wildly popular and unfortunately caused lasting confusion about what "Mahjong" means in Western contexts.
- True four-player computer Mahjong games became common in Japan during the 1980s, particularly on the NEC PC-88 and PC-98 platforms.

### The Online Revolution

The internet transformed Mahjong from a local, in-person game to a global competitive activity:

- **Early 2000s**: Websites like Tenhou (2006), Ron2, and various Chinese platforms launched, enabling anonymous online play.
- **2010s**: Mobile apps democratized access further. Mahjong Soul's anime-styled approach and global marketing brought in players who might never have visited a Mahjong parlor.
- **Social features**: Online platforms added friend lists, clubs, spectating, and replay sharing, creating vibrant communities.

### Mahjong Soul (Jantama)

Launched in 2019 by Yostar (publishing) and Cat Food Studio (development), **Mahjong Soul** has become the most significant global Mahjong platform of the modern era:

- Anime-styled character system with collectible characters
- Free-to-play model with cosmetic monetization
- Available on mobile and web browser
- Hosted major tournaments with significant prize pools
- Growing competitive scene with regular seasonal events
- Credited with dramatically increasing Western interest in Riichi Mahjong

### AI Achievements

Mahjong has proven to be a fascinating challenge for artificial intelligence:

- **Suphx (2019)**: Developed by Microsoft Research Asia, Suphx became the first AI to achieve the rank of "10-dan" on Tenhou's four-player Mahjong ladder. This was a landmark achievement because Mahjong's imperfect information (hidden tiles), four-player dynamics, and complex scoring make it fundamentally different from — and in some ways harder than — games like chess or Go where AI had already surpassed human ability.

- **Key challenges** for Mahjong AI:
  - Partial observability (you cannot see other players' hands)
  - Multi-agent dynamics (three opponents, not just one)
  - Stochastic elements (random draws from the wall)
  - Complex scoring interactions
  - Defensive play requiring opponent modeling

- **NAGA**: Another AI system that achieved strong results on Tenhou and became available as an analysis tool for human players. NAGA's evaluations are used by competitive players to review their games and identify mistakes.

- **Impact on strategy**: AI analysis has confirmed and refined many human strategic principles while also revealing counterintuitive plays that humans had not previously considered. For example, AI analysis showed that some traditionally "safe" plays were actually suboptimal in terms of expected value.

## The Future

### Growing Global Community

Riichi Mahjong's global community continues to grow steadily:

- European tournaments regularly attract hundreds of competitors from dozens of countries.
- North American Mahjong clubs and tournaments are increasing in number and size.
- Online platforms report year-over-year growth in player counts.
- Content creators (streamers, YouTubers, bloggers) are producing Mahjong educational and entertainment content in many languages.

### AI as Teacher and Opponent

AI tools are increasingly serving as educational aids:

- AI analysis of game replays helps players identify mistakes and improve.
- AI opponents of calibrated difficulty levels help beginners learn at their own pace.
- AI tutoring systems can explain why certain moves are good or bad in real-time.
- The integration of large language models with Mahjong AI opens possibilities for natural-language coaching.

### Esports Potential

With the M-League in Japan demonstrating that professional Mahjong can sustain broadcast audiences and corporate sponsorship, there is potential for similar leagues internationally:

- The combination of skill, drama, and spectator-friendly moments (riichi declarations, big wins, dramatic defenses) makes Mahjong well-suited for competitive broadcasting.
- Online platforms with built-in spectating and tournament features reduce the infrastructure barriers to organized competition.
- The four-player format creates unique viewing dynamics compared to head-to-head esports.

### Accessibility and Inclusion

Modern Mahjong development focuses on making the game accessible:

- Multi-language support in online platforms
- Tutorial systems and guided play for beginners
- Colorblind-friendly tile designs
- AI opponents that adapt to player skill levels
- Simplified rule variants for introduction to new players

### The Enduring Appeal

Despite being over 150 years old, Mahjong continues to attract new players worldwide. Its combination of strategic depth, social interaction, cultural richness, and sheer tactile pleasure ensures that the game will remain vital for generations to come. Whether played at a family gathering during Chinese New Year, in a competitive tournament in Europe, in an online match connecting players across continents, or against AI opponents in a digital game, Mahjong's fundamental appeal — the challenge of building something complete from random pieces, the thrill of reading opponents, and the joy of hearing tiles click together — endures.

## Interesting Facts

- The total number of possible 13-tile starting hands in Mahjong is approximately 326 billion, making the game's variety virtually limitless.
- The fastest recorded legitimate Mahjong win happened on the dealer's first discard — a phenomenon called "renhou" in some rulesets, though whether this counts as a yakuman varies.
- In 2023, the term "Mahjong" was officially recognized in the Oxford English Dictionary, joining "kung fu" and "dim sum" as Chinese-origin words in standard English.
- The most expensive Mahjong set ever sold at auction was a 19th-century set with ivory tiles and a rosewood case, which fetched over $100,000.
- During the 1920s American craze, the demand for Mahjong tiles was so great that the supply of cattle shinbones in Chicago was temporarily depleted.
- In parts of China, it is said that the sound of Mahjong tiles is one of the "four great sounds of happiness" along with a baby's cry, rain on a tin roof, and the sound of coins.
