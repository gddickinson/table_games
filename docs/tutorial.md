## Chapter 1: What is Mahjong?

**Learning Objective:** Understand what Mahjong is, where it comes from, and why millions of people love playing it.

### What Kind of Game Is This?

Mahjong is a tile-based game for four players that originated in China during the 19th century. Think of it as a blend between rummy (forming sets and runs) and poker (reading opponents and managing risk). Instead of cards, you play with beautifully engraved tiles that feel satisfying to shuffle, stack, and click together.

### How Is It Like Card Games?

If you have played Gin Rummy, you already understand the core concept:

| Rummy Concept | Mahjong Equivalent |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Drawing a card | Drawing a tile from the wall |
| Discarding a card | Discarding a tile to your river |
| Forming runs (3-4-5) | Forming sequences/chows (3m-4m-5m) |
| Forming sets (K-K-K) | Forming triplets/pongs (5p-5p-5p) |
| Going out | Declaring a win (tsumo or ron) |

The key differences: Mahjong has four players instead of two, you can claim other players' discards under specific rules, and the scoring system rewards elegant and difficult hand compositions.

### Why Is Mahjong Fun?

- **Every hand is a puzzle.** You start with 13 random tiles and must find the best path to a winning hand.
- **Social interaction.** The claiming system means you are constantly watching and reacting to other players.
- **Risk management.** Do you push for a high-scoring hand or settle for a safe, quick win? Do you attack or defend?
- **Depth.** Beginners can enjoy the basics, but there is enough strategic depth to keep you learning for years.
- **Cultural richness.** The tiles are beautiful, the terminology is evocative, and playing connects you to a centuries-old tradition.

### Common Mistakes (Before You Even Start)

- **Confusing Mahjong with Mahjong Solitaire.** The single-player tile-matching computer game is NOT Mahjong. Real Mahjong is a four-player competitive game.
- **Thinking it is pure luck.** While there is a luck component (as in poker), skilled players win significantly more over time.
- **Being overwhelmed by scoring.** You do not need to memorize all scoring patterns to play. Start with the basics and learn as you go.

### Practice Exercise

Before moving on, familiarize yourself with the game interface. Open a practice game, look at the tiles in your hand, and notice the wall, the discard areas, and the other players' positions. Do not worry about playing yet — just look around.

## Chapter 2: Meet the Tiles

**Learning Objective:** Recognize all tile types by sight and understand how they are categorized.

### The Three Suits

Mahjong has three suits, each numbered 1 through 9. There are four copies of every tile, giving 108 suited tiles total.

#### Characters (Manzu)

Characters display Chinese numerals with the character for "ten thousand" (wan/man).

```
 1m    2m    3m    4m    5m    6m    7m    8m    9m
[一]  [二]  [三]  [四]  [五]  [六]  [七]  [八]  [九]
 万    万    万    万    万    万    万    万    万
```

> **Beginner Tip:** Characters can be hard to read at first because they use Chinese numerals. The game displays the number in the corner of each tile to help you.

#### Circles (Pinzu)

Circles show the indicated number of circles (dots/coins). These are the easiest to read.

```
 1p    2p    3p    4p    5p    6p    7p    8p    9p
 (o)  (oo) (ooo)(oooo) (X)  (ooo)(ooo)(oooo)(ooo)
              o    oo   oXo   ooo  oooo  oooo  ooo
                               o    o          ooo
```

#### Bamboo (Souzu)

Bamboo tiles show sticks of bamboo in the indicated number. The 1 of Bamboo is special — it is usually depicted as a bird (sparrow) rather than a single stick.

```
 1s    2s    3s    4s    5s    6s    7s    8s    9s
[鳥]  [||]  [|||] [||]  [|||] [|||] [|||] [||||][|||]
             [|]   [||]  [||]  [|||] [|||] [||||][|||]
                                [|]        [||]  [|||]
```

### Honor Tiles

Honor tiles cannot form sequences — only triplets and quads.

#### The Four Winds

```
 East    South    West    North
 [東]     [南]    [西]     [北]
```

Winds are significant because each player has a "seat wind" and each round has a "round wind." Collecting a triplet of a relevant wind scores bonus points.

#### The Three Dragons

```
 White      Green      Red
 [    ]     [發]       [中]
 (blank)  (green)    (red)
```

Dragon triplets always score bonus points. Collecting all three is one of the highest-scoring combinations in the game.

### Terminal vs. Simple Tiles

This distinction matters for many scoring patterns:

- **Terminals:** The 1 and 9 of each suit (1m, 9m, 1p, 9p, 1s, 9s). Six types.
- **Simples:** Tiles numbered 2 through 8 in each suit. Twenty-one types.
- **Honors:** All wind and dragon tiles. Seven types.

```
Terminals:  1m 9m 1p 9p 1s 9s        (edge tiles)
Simples:    2m 3m 4m 5m 6m 7m 8m     (middle tiles)
            2p 3p ... 8p
            2s 3s ... 8s
Honors:     Ew Sw Ww Nw Wd Gd Rd     (wind + dragon)
```

### How Many Tiles?

| Category | Unique Types | Copies Each | Total |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Characters | 9 | 4 | 36 |
| Circles | 9 | 4 | 36 |
| Bamboo | 9 | 4 | 36 |
| Winds | 4 | 4 | 16 |
| Dragons | 3 | 4 | 12 |
| **Total** | **34** | | **136** |

> **Key Point:** There are exactly 4 copies of each tile. This means the maximum number of any specific tile you can ever see is 4. Tracking how many copies have been played is a fundamental skill.

### Common Mistakes

- Mixing up Characters and Bamboo when you are new. Focus on the overall look: Characters have Chinese writing, Bamboo has sticks/tubes.
- Forgetting that the 1 of Bamboo looks like a bird, not a single stick.
- Trying to make sequences with honor tiles. Winds and dragons can only form triplets.

### Practice Exercise

1. Sort your hand by suit. Put all Characters together, all Circles together, all Bamboo together, and honors at the end.
2. Identify every tile in your hand by name. "This is a 3 of Circles, this is a West Wind..."
3. Count how many terminals, simples, and honors you have.

## Chapter 3: Your First Hand

**Learning Objective:** Understand what a winning hand looks like and how to build one.

### The 4+1 Pattern

Almost every winning hand in Mahjong follows this structure:

```
4 MELDS + 1 PAIR = WIN

A meld is either:
  - A SEQUENCE (chow):  three consecutive tiles in one suit  (e.g., 4p 5p 6p)
  - A TRIPLET (pong):   three identical tiles               (e.g., 8s 8s 8s)

A pair is:
  - Two identical tiles                                      (e.g., Rd Rd)
```

Total tiles: (3 + 3 + 3 + 3) + 2 = **14 tiles**

### Example Winning Hands

**Example 1: All sequences**
```
[1m 2m 3m] [4p 5p 6p] [7s 8s 9s] [2m 3m 4m] + [Nw Nw]
 sequence    sequence    sequence    sequence     pair
```

**Example 2: Mixed sequences and triplets**
```
[3p 3p 3p] [5s 6s 7s] [Ew Ew Ew] [1m 2m 3m] + [9p 9p]
 triplet     sequence    triplet    sequence     pair
```

**Example 3: All triplets**
```
[2m 2m 2m] [6p 6p 6p] [Rd Rd Rd] [8s 8s 8s] + [Ww Ww]
 triplet     triplet    triplet    triplet      pair
```

### Building Toward a Win

You start with 13 tiles. Each turn you draw one tile (making 14) and discard one (back to 13). Your goal is to arrange your tiles so that when you draw your 14th tile (or claim someone's discard), all 14 tiles form the 4+1 pattern.

**Starting hand example:**
```
1m 3m 5m 5m 5m 2p 4p 8p 8p 3s 6s 7s Ew
```

Looking at this hand, you might see the beginnings of several melds:
- `5m 5m 5m` — already a complete triplet!
- `6s 7s` — needs a `5s` or `8s` to complete a sequence
- `8p 8p` — could become a pair or work toward a triplet
- `1m 3m` — needs a `2m` to complete a sequence
- `2p 4p` — needs a `3p` to complete a sequence
- `Ew` — isolated, unless you draw more East Wind tiles

### Shanten: How Far From Winning?

**Shanten** is the number of tiles you need to draw (and exchange) before you are one tile away from winning.

- **Tenpai (0-shanten):** You need exactly 1 more tile to win. You are "ready."
- **1-shanten:** You need to make 1 more improvement before reaching tenpai.
- **2-shanten:** You need 2 more improvements.

> **Beginner Goal:** In each hand, try to reduce your shanten count by one each turn. If you start at 4-shanten, aim to reach tenpai before the wall runs out.

### Common Mistakes

- **Tunnel vision on one meld.** Do not fixate on completing one specific meld while ignoring the rest of your hand.
- **Keeping isolated honor tiles too long.** A single wind tile with no partner is usually worth discarding early.
- **Forgetting the pair.** You need exactly one pair. Make sure your plan includes one.

### Practice Exercise

1. Look at your starting hand and identify any complete melds.
2. Identify partial melds (two tiles that need a third).
3. Identify isolated tiles that do not connect to anything.
4. Try to imagine what your finished hand could look like.

## Chapter 4: Playing a Turn

**Learning Objective:** Execute the draw-evaluate-discard cycle confidently.

### Step 1: Draw

At the start of your turn, take the next tile from the wall. The game does this automatically — the new tile appears at the right end of your hand (or highlighted).

### Step 2: Evaluate

Now you have 14 tiles. Ask yourself:

1. **Is this a winning tile?** If your 14 tiles form a complete hand (4 melds + 1 pair), you can declare a win! (We cover this in Chapter 6.)
2. **Does this tile improve my hand?** Does it complete a partial meld or create a new useful connection?
3. **Is this tile useless?** Does it not connect to anything in my hand?

### The Evaluation Checklist

```
[ ] Do I have a complete winning hand? → Declare win
[ ] Can I form a concealed kong? → Consider declaring it
[ ] Does the new tile improve my hand? → Keep it, discard something worse
[ ] Is the new tile useless? → Discard it immediately
[ ] Am I keeping too many isolated tiles? → Discard the least useful one
```

### Step 3: Discard

Choose one tile from your 14 and place it in your discard area (river). Click the tile you want to discard, or drag it to the discard area.

**What to discard?** Here is a priority guide for beginners:

1. **Isolated honor tiles** — A single wind or dragon with no partner is usually the first to go (unless it is your seat wind, round wind, or a dragon — those have inherent value).
2. **Isolated terminals** — A lone 1 or 9 with no neighboring tiles is less useful than middle tiles.
3. **Redundant tiles** — If you have four tiles in the same area (like 3m-4m-5m-6m), you may not need all of them.
4. **Keep pairs and partial sequences** — Two connected tiles (like 5p-6p) are valuable because they are close to completing a meld.

### Reading Your Hand

Here is how to think about tile relationships:

```
Connected pairs (good - keep these):
  5p 6p  → needs 4p or 7p (two-sided wait, 8 possible tiles)
  3s 3s  → pair, might become triplet (2 possible tiles)

One-gap connections (decent):
  2m 4m  → needs 3m (closed wait, 4 possible tiles)

Isolated tiles (candidates for discard):
  Nw     → needs 2 more North Wind (unlikely)
  1p     → needs 2p or another 1p nearby
```

### Turn Timing

In this game, you have a time limit per turn (configurable in settings). The timer appears on screen. If time runs out, the game auto-discards a tile for you (typically the drawn tile).

> **Tip:** Early in a round, take your time to plan. As you get more experienced, turns will get faster naturally.

### Common Mistakes

- **Auto-discarding the drawn tile.** New players often just throw back whatever they drew. Always check if the new tile is more useful than something already in your hand.
- **Hoarding honor tiles.** Keeping three different single wind tiles "just in case" wastes hand space. Unless you are going for a specific hand pattern, let most of them go.
- **Not sorting your hand.** Keep tiles organized by suit and value. It helps you spot connections.

### Practice Exercise

1. Play 3 practice rounds focusing ONLY on the draw-evaluate-discard cycle.
2. Before each discard, verbally (or mentally) say why you are discarding that tile.
3. Try to reduce your shanten count by at least 1 per turn.

## Chapter 5: Claiming Tiles

**Learning Objective:** Know when and how to claim other players' discards, and understand the trade-offs.

### What Is Claiming?

When another player discards a tile, you can take that tile to immediately complete a meld in your hand. This is called **claiming** (or calling).

### Types of Claims

| Claim | What You Form | Who Can Claim | What You Reveal |
|-------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Chi** (Chow) | A sequence | Only the next player in turn order | 2 tiles completing the sequence |
| **Pon** (Pong) | A triplet | Any player | 2 matching tiles from your hand |
| **Kan** (Kong) | A quad | Any player | 3 matching tiles from your hand |
| **Ron** (Win) | Your winning hand | Any player | Your entire hand |

### How Claiming Works

1. When someone discards a tile you want, a prompt appears on screen.
2. Click the claim button (or press the keyboard shortcut).
3. If claiming a chow, you may need to select which tiles form the sequence (if multiple options exist).
4. Your completed meld appears face-up to the side of your hand.
5. You then discard a tile (unless you claimed a win).

### The Big Trade-Off: Open vs. Closed

This is the most important concept in claiming strategy:

```
CLAIMING (Opening Your Hand):
  + Completes a meld immediately (no waiting to draw the tile)
  + Gets you closer to winning faster

  - Your hand becomes "open" — visible melds give opponents information
  - Many scoring patterns require a closed hand or are worth fewer points when open
  - You cannot declare riichi with an open hand
  - Reduces your overall hand value in most cases
```

> **Beginner Rule of Thumb:** Only claim tiles when:
> 1. You are claiming a win (ron) — always do this.
> 2. You are claiming a valuable triplet (dragon pong, wind pong) that gives you a scoring pattern.
> 3. You are very close to winning and the speed advantage outweighs the value loss.

### Claiming Priority

If multiple players want the same discarded tile:

```
Ron (Win)   →  Highest priority — always wins
Pon (Pong)  →  Beats chow claims
Chi (Chow)  →  Lowest priority — only if no one else claims
```

### When NOT to Claim

- **Early in the round** when you are far from tenpai. Opening your hand for a single chow when you still need 3 more melds is rarely worth it.
- **When it would kill your hand value.** If you have a potential riichi + pinfu hand (worth significant points), opening it for a chow drops the value dramatically.
- **When the discard would give you information.** Sometimes the tile is more valuable as intelligence (tracking what has been discarded) than as part of your hand.

### Common Mistakes

- **Claiming every chow possible.** This is the number one beginner mistake. Chows are easy to form naturally through drawing. Save your claims for valuable melds.
- **Forgetting that claiming reveals information.** When you call pon on two red dragons, every opponent now knows you are collecting dragons and will avoid discarding the third.
- **Not considering what you will discard after claiming.** Before claiming, think about what tile you will discard afterward and whether that discard is safe.

### Practice Exercise

1. Play 3 rounds WITHOUT claiming anything (except wins). Notice how your hand develops naturally.
2. Play 3 more rounds allowing yourself to claim ONLY pongs of dragons or your seat wind.
3. Compare how the games feel. In which did you win more? In which did you score higher?

## Chapter 6: Your First Win

**Learning Objective:** Recognize when you are tenpai (ready) and successfully declare a win.

### Recognizing Tenpai

You are tenpai when your hand needs exactly one more tile to complete the 4 melds + 1 pair structure. The game will highlight your hand when you reach tenpai (with hints enabled).

**Example tenpai hand:**
```
[1m 2m 3m] [5p 5p 5p] [7s 8s 9s] [Ew Ew Ew]  3m ___
                                                     ^ need pair tile
```
This hand is waiting for `3m` to complete the pair. If you draw `3m` or someone discards `3m`, you can win.

### Common Wait Patterns

Understanding wait types helps you see tenpai faster:

**Two-sided wait (Ryanmen) — the best wait:**
```
Hand: ... 4s 5s ...
Waiting for: 3s or 6s (8 possible tiles)
```

**Closed wait (Kanchan):**
```
Hand: ... 2p 4p ...
Waiting for: 3p (4 possible tiles)
```

**Edge wait (Penchan):**
```
Hand: ... 8m 9m ...
Waiting for: 7m (4 possible tiles)
```

**Pair wait (Tanki):**
```
Hand: ... 6s ...  (everything else is in complete melds)
Waiting for: 6s (3 possible tiles)
```

**Dual triplet wait (Shanpon):**
```
Hand: ... 2m 2m ... 8p 8p ...  (other melds complete)
Waiting for: 2m or 8p (4 possible tiles)
```

### Declaring a Win

#### Tsumo (Self-Draw Win)

If you draw your winning tile from the wall:
1. The game highlights the winning tile and shows a "Tsumo" button.
2. Click "Tsumo" (or press the keyboard shortcut).
3. Your hand is revealed and scored.
4. All three opponents pay you.

#### Ron (Claim Win)

If another player discards your winning tile:
1. The game highlights the discard and shows a "Ron" button.
2. Click "Ron" (or press the keyboard shortcut).
3. Your hand is revealed and scored.
4. Only the player who discarded pays you.

### The Yaku Requirement

In Riichi Mahjong, your winning hand must include at least one **yaku** (scoring pattern). A structurally complete hand with no yaku is not a valid win.

Do not worry about memorizing all yaku yet. For beginners, focus on these five easy yaku:

| Yaku | How to Get It | Tips |
|------|--------------|------|
| **Riichi** | Declare riichi when tenpai with closed hand | Easiest yaku — just keep your hand closed |
| **Tanyao** | All tiles are simples (2-8), no terminals or honors | Discard all 1s, 9s, winds, and dragons |
| **Yakuhai** | Pong of dragons, seat wind, or round wind | Easy when you start with a pair of dragons |
| **Menzen Tsumo** | Win by self-draw with a closed hand | Just draw your winning tile yourself, no claims |
| **Pinfu** | Closed hand, all sequences, two-sided wait, non-scoring pair | Requires some planning |

### Your First Win Checklist

```
Before declaring a win, verify:
[ ] My hand has 4 melds + 1 pair (or seven pairs / thirteen orphans)
[ ] My hand has at least 1 yaku
[ ] I am NOT in furiten (none of my winning tiles are in my discard pool)
```

> **In this game, the "Win" button only appears when you have a valid winning hand, so you cannot accidentally declare an invalid win.** But understanding the requirements will help your strategy.

### Common Mistakes

- **Not recognizing tenpai.** Practice identifying when you are one tile away. Look for incomplete melds and missing pairs.
- **Missing a win opportunity.** If an opponent discards your winning tile and you do not notice in time, the chance passes. Stay alert, especially when you are tenpai.
- **Declaring riichi when you have no yaku without it.** This is actually correct! Riichi itself IS a yaku. But make sure you meet the riichi requirements (closed hand, 1000+ points).

### Practice Exercise

1. Play practice rounds with hints enabled (the game will tell you when you are tenpai).
2. Each time you reach tenpai, pause and identify: what tile(s) are you waiting for? What type of wait is it?
3. Try to win at least once by tsumo and once by ron.

## Chapter 7: Scoring Basics

**Learning Objective:** Understand the five most important scoring patterns and roughly how much they are worth.

### How Scoring Works (Simplified)

Mahjong scoring has two components:

1. **Han (Yaku)** — Each scoring pattern adds han. More han = exponentially more points.
2. **Fu (Minipoints)** — Fine-grained bonuses for specific tile combinations and wait types.

> **Beginner Approach:** Focus on han. More han = more points. Do not worry about fu calculations until you are comfortable with the basics.

### The Points Ladder

| Han | Approximate Points (Non-dealer) | Name |
|-----|----------------------------------|------|
| 1 han | 1,000 - 2,000 | Minimum hand |
| 2 han | 2,000 - 3,900 | Small hand |
| 3 han | 3,900 - 7,700 | Medium hand |
| 4 han | 7,700 - 8,000 | Large hand |
| 5 han | 8,000 | **Mangan** (limit) |
| 6-7 han | 12,000 | **Haneman** |
| 8-10 han | 16,000 | **Baiman** |
| 11-12 han | 24,000 | **Sanbaiman** |
| 13+ han | 32,000 | **Yakuman** |

### Top 5 Scoring Patterns Every Beginner Should Know

#### 1. Riichi (1 han)

Declare "riichi" when your hand is closed (no claims) and tenpai (one tile away from winning).

```
Closed tenpai hand + riichi declaration = riichi yaku

Bonus: If you win within one turn cycle, you also get ippatsu (+1 han)
Bonus: When you win, ura dora indicators may add more han
```

**Why it matters:** Riichi is the most common yaku. It is essentially free — just keep your hand closed and declare when ready.

#### 2. Tanyao (1 han) — All Simples

Your entire hand consists of tiles numbered 2 through 8. No terminals (1, 9), no honors (winds, dragons).

```
Valid:   [2m 3m 4m] [5p 5p 5p] [6s 7s 8s] [3m 4m 5m] + [8p 8p]
Invalid: [1m 2m 3m] ...  (contains a terminal: 1m)
```

**Why it matters:** Tanyao hands are easy to form because simple tiles are abundant. They combine well with other yaku.

#### 3. Yakuhai (1 han each) — Value Tiles

A triplet (pong) of any of the following:
- Any dragon tile (White, Green, Red)
- The current round wind (e.g., East Wind during the East round)
- Your current seat wind

```
[Rd Rd Rd] = 1 han  (Red Dragon triplet)
[Ew Ew Ew] = 1 han  (East Wind triplet, if East is round wind)
[Ew Ew Ew] = 1 han  (East Wind triplet, if you are sitting East)
[Ew Ew Ew] = 2 han  (East Wind, if BOTH round wind AND your seat)
```

**Why it matters:** Yakuhai works even with an open hand, making it the easiest way to get yaku when claiming tiles.

#### 4. Menzen Tsumo (1 han) — Concealed Self-Draw

Win by drawing your winning tile yourself (tsumo) while your hand is fully concealed (no open melds).

```
Closed hand + self-drawn winning tile = menzen tsumo
```

**Why it matters:** You get this "for free" whenever you draw your winning tile with a closed hand. It stacks with riichi for an easy 2+ han hand.

#### 5. Pinfu (1 han) — All Sequences

Requirements (all must be met):
- Closed hand
- All four melds are sequences (no triplets)
- Your wait is a two-sided wait (ryanmen)
- Your pair is NOT a scoring tile (not dragons, not seat wind, not round wind)

```
[1m 2m 3m] [4p 5p 6p] [2s 3s 4s] [6s 7s 8s] + [3m 3m]
All sequences, two-sided wait, non-scoring pair = pinfu
```

**Why it matters:** Pinfu is the foundation of efficient hands. It combines beautifully with riichi, tanyao, and iipeikou for high-value hands.

### Combining Yaku

Yaku stack! A single hand can have multiple yaku:

```
Riichi + Pinfu + Tanyao + Menzen Tsumo + Ippatsu + Dora
1 han  + 1 han + 1 han  + 1 han        + 1 han   + varies

Even a simple combination like Riichi + Tanyao + Pinfu = 3 han ≈ 3,900+ points
```

### Common Mistakes

- **Ignoring yaku requirements.** A hand with no yaku cannot win, no matter how perfectly structured.
- **Not knowing which yaku your hand has.** Before declaring riichi or waiting, identify your yaku. The game shows your yaku in the hint panel if enabled.
- **Undervaluing dora.** Dora tiles add han without being yaku themselves. A hand with riichi + 3 dora is mangan (8,000 points)!

### Practice Exercise

1. Win a hand with riichi. Just keep your hand closed and declare when tenpai.
2. Win a hand with tanyao. Actively discard all terminals and honors.
3. Win a hand with yakuhai. Look for dragon or wind pairs and try to form triplets.

## Chapter 8: Riichi

**Learning Objective:** Know when to declare riichi, understand its benefits and risks, and use it effectively.

### What Riichi Does

When you declare riichi, you are telling the table: "I am one tile away from winning, and I am locking in my hand."

**Benefits:**
- Riichi is worth 1 han (minimum requirement to win).
- Ippatsu chance: winning within one turn cycle adds 1 han.
- Ura dora: if you win, tiles beneath the dora indicators are revealed for bonus han.
- Intimidation: opponents may play defensively, giving you more chances to draw your tile.

**Costs:**
- You pay a 1,000-point deposit (returned only if you win the round).
- Your hand is locked — you cannot change your tiles.
- You reveal that you are tenpai, alerting opponents.
- You cannot claim chow or pong (only ron for the win).

### When to Declare Riichi

**Good times to riichi:**
- You have a good wait (two-sided wait with many possible tiles).
- It is early in the round (plenty of wall tiles remaining).
- Your hand has no other yaku (riichi provides the minimum yaku you need).
- You are behind in score and need points.
- Ura dora could significantly increase your hand value.

**Bad times to riichi:**
- Your wait is bad (single tile, only 1-2 tiles remaining in play).
- The round is almost over (few wall tiles left).
- You already have enough yaku and good hand value without riichi.
- You are in first place with a comfortable lead (riichi deposit is unnecessary risk).
- Multiple opponents are also tenpai or showing dangerous signs.

### Riichi Timing: Early vs. Late

| Timing | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| **Early riichi** (first 6 turns) | Maximum ippatsu window, more wall tiles to draw, opponents have discarded fewer safe tiles | Opponents have full hands to defend with |
| **Late riichi** (after turn 12+) | Opponents may have fewer safe tiles to discard | Fewer wall tiles remaining, lower ippatsu chance |

### Damaten (Hidden Tenpai)

The alternative to riichi is **damaten** — staying tenpai without declaring. Your hand remains hidden, and you can still win by ron or tsumo.

**Use damaten when:**
- Your hand already has sufficient yaku and value.
- You want to keep flexibility (you can change your hand if needed).
- The element of surprise is more valuable than the extra han.

### Common Mistakes

- **Always declaring riichi when tenpai.** Riichi is powerful but not always correct. Sometimes staying silent is better.
- **Declaring riichi with a bad wait.** Riichi with a single-tile wait and 0 remaining copies is almost guaranteed to fail.
- **Forgetting the 1,000-point requirement.** You cannot declare riichi if you have fewer than 1,000 points.

### Practice Exercise

1. Play 5 rounds and always declare riichi when eligible. Track your win rate.
2. Play 5 rounds and never declare riichi (stay damaten). Track your win rate.
3. Compare the results and think about when each approach worked better.

## Chapter 9: Defense 101

**Learning Objective:** Identify safe tiles, recognize danger, and know when to abandon your hand to avoid dealing into an opponent's win.

### Why Defense Matters

In Mahjong, the player who discards the winning tile pays the full price (ron). A single careless discard can cost you 8,000+ points. Learning to defend is just as important as learning to attack.

### The Defensive Mindset

After an opponent declares riichi (or shows signs of tenpai), ask yourself:

```
1. How close am I to winning?
   → Very close (tenpai or 1-shanten): Consider pushing
   → Far away (2+ shanten): Consider folding

2. How valuable would my win be?
   → High value: More reason to push
   → Low value: Not worth the risk

3. How dangerous are my discards?
   → Safe tiles available: I can defend while still drawing
   → No safe tiles: Folding may require giving up melds
```

### Safe Tile Categories

#### Genbutsu (100% Safe)

Tiles that an opponent has already discarded are **completely safe** against that specific opponent. They cannot win on a tile they previously discarded (due to the furiten rule).

```
Opponent's discards: 3m, 7p, Nw, 5s, 2m, 8p
Safe tiles against this opponent: 3m, 7p, Nw, 5s, 2m, 8p
```

> **This is the most important defensive concept.** Memorize it: if they discarded it, they cannot ron it.

#### Suji (Reasonably Safe)

Suji is based on the logic that two-sided waits come in pairs. If an opponent has discarded `4m`, then `1m` and `7m` are relatively safer because:
- A two-sided wait on `1m` would be `2m-3m` (waiting for `1m` or `4m`) — but they discarded `4m`, so this wait would be furiten.
- Similarly, a wait on `7m` from `5m-6m` would also need `4m`.

```
Suji pairs:
1-4:  If 4 is discarded, 1 is safer
2-5:  If 5 is discarded, 2 is safer
3-6:  If 6 is discarded, 3 is safer
4-7:  If 4 is discarded, 7 is safer (same as 1-4 reversed)
5-8:  If 5 is discarded, 8 is safer
6-9:  If 6 is discarded, 9 is safer
```

> **Warning:** Suji is not 100% safe. It only protects against two-sided waits, not closed waits, edge waits, or single tile waits.

#### Kabe (Wall / No-Chance)

If all 4 copies of a tile are visible (discarded or in open melds), tiles that would require that tile for a sequence are safer.

```
All four 5p are visible →
  No one can have [4p 5p] waiting for 3p or 6p
  No one can have [5p 6p] waiting for 4p or 7p
  No one can have [4p 6p] waiting for 5p (obviously)
```

### When to Fold

**Fold (play defensively) when:**
- An opponent declares riichi and you are far from tenpai.
- Two opponents appear to be tenpai.
- Your hand value is low (not worth the risk).
- You are in a strong score position and do not need to win this round.

**Folding means:**
- Discard only safe tiles (genbutsu first, then suji, then kabe).
- Break apart your own melds if necessary to find safe discards.
- Accept that you will not win this round.

### Common Mistakes

- **Never folding.** Some beginners always push for their hand regardless of danger. This leads to paying many large ron penalties.
- **Over-folding.** Some players become too defensive and never win. Balance is key.
- **Not tracking discards.** You cannot use genbutsu or suji if you are not paying attention to what has been discarded.

### Practice Exercise

1. Play a round and actively fold whenever an opponent declares riichi. Focus only on safe tile selection.
2. Track whether you dealt into any opponent wins. How many points did you save?
3. Practice identifying suji relationships for 5 minutes: given a discarded tile, what tiles become safer?

## Chapter 10: Tile Efficiency

**Learning Objective:** Make better discard decisions by understanding tile acceptance and optimal hand development.

### What Is Tile Efficiency?

Tile efficiency is the art of maximizing the number of tiles that could improve your hand. The more tiles that can help you, the faster you reach tenpai.

### Ukeire (Acceptance Count)

Ukeire is the number of tiles that would reduce your shanten count (bring you closer to tenpai).

**Example:**
```
Hand: 2m 3m | 5p 6p | 1s 2s | 7s 8s | Ew Ew | Rd | Nw

If I discard Nw, my acceptance is:
  1m or 4m (completes 2m-3m) = 8 tiles
  4p or 7p (completes 5p-6p) = 8 tiles
  3s (completes 1s-2s-3s) = 4 tiles
  6s or 9s (completes 7s-8s) = 8 tiles
  Ew (improves pair to triplet) = 2 tiles
  Rd (gives Rd a pair) = 3 tiles
  Total: ~33 possible useful tiles

If I discard Rd instead, similar calculation but keeping Nw...
```

The discard that maximizes your total ukeire is the most "efficient" choice.

### Key Principles

#### 1. Two-Sided Connections Are King

A pair of consecutive tiles like `4p-5p` accepts tiles from both sides (`3p` and `6p`). This gives you 8 possible improving tiles (4 copies of each). Always prefer two-sided connections over one-sided ones.

```
Best:   4p 5p  (waits for 3p or 6p = 8 tiles)
OK:     1p 2p  (waits for 3p only = 4 tiles, edge)
OK:     3p 5p  (waits for 4p only = 4 tiles, closed)
```

#### 2. Pairs Are Flexible

A pair can become a triplet (if you draw the third copy) or serve as your final pair. Keep at least one pair in your hand, but having too many pairs creates inefficiency.

```
Good: 1-2 pairs (flexible)
Bad:  4+ pairs (hard to form melds from all of them)
```

#### 3. Isolated Tiles Have Low Value

A tile with no connection to any other tile in your hand has zero ukeire contribution. Discard these first.

```
Connected:  5m in a hand with 4m and 6m → high value
Isolated:   Nw with no other North Wind → low value (only 3 possible improving tiles)
```

#### 4. The Middle Is More Efficient Than the Edges

Middle tiles (3-7) can form more connections than edge tiles (1, 2, 8, 9).

```
5p can be part of: 3-4-5, 4-5-6, 5-6-7 (three possible sequences)
1p can be part of: 1-2-3 (only one possible sequence)
```

### Shanten Calculation

To calculate your shanten:

```
Shanten = (4 - number_of_complete_melds) - (number_of_partial_melds)
          ... with adjustments for pairs and overlap
```

The game displays your shanten count (with hints enabled). For manual calculation, count your melds and partial melds, then determine how many more improvements you need.

### Common Mistakes

- **Chasing a specific tile instead of maximizing acceptance.** If you are waiting on 1 tile versus 8 tiles, the 8-tile option is almost always better.
- **Breaking useful connections for marginal gains.** Do not discard a tile from a two-sided pair to chase a slightly better theoretical hand.
- **Ignoring defensive considerations.** Pure efficiency ignores danger. The "most efficient" discard might also be the most dangerous one.

### Practice Exercise

1. At the start of a hand, count the useful tiles for each possible discard.
2. Choose the discard that leaves the most useful tiles.
3. Over 10 hands, compare how quickly you reach tenpai using this approach versus intuition.

## Chapter 11: Reading the Table

**Learning Objective:** Extract information from opponents' discards and open melds to make better decisions.

### The Discard Pool Tells a Story

Every tile an opponent discards is a clue. The order and composition of discards reveals their strategy.

### What Early Discards Tell You

Tiles discarded in the first 3-4 turns are usually:
- **Isolated tiles** the player does not need
- **Tiles far from their hand's direction**

```
Early discards: Nw, Ew, 1s, 9p
→ This player likely has a hand focused on simples (tanyao direction)
→ They are NOT collecting winds
→ They probably do not have terminal-heavy suits
```

### What Late Discards Tell You

Tiles discarded after turn 8-10 are more revealing:
- **Previously useful tiles** the player has now given up on
- **Tiles cut from completed shape** as the hand nears tenpai
- **Dangerous tiles** that became safe (the player was holding them for safety)

```
Early discards: Nw, 9m, 1p
Late discards:  3s, 4s (turn 10-11)
→ The player probably had 3s-4s as a partial sequence but abandoned it
→ They likely found a better connection and are getting close to tenpai
```

### Open Melds Reveal Everything

When a player claims a tile and forms an open meld, you gain enormous information:

```
Player has open melds: [Rd Rd Rd] [Gd Gd Gd]
→ They have two dragon triplets
→ If they get a White Dragon triplet, that is Big Three Dragons (yakuman!)
→ NEVER discard White Dragon toward this player
→ Even a White Dragon pair gives them Little Three Dragons (high value)
```

### Tedashi vs. Tsumogiri

- **Tedashi**: Discarding a tile from the hand (not the one just drawn).
- **Tsumogiri**: Discarding the tile that was just drawn.

```
If a player draws and immediately discards (tsumogiri):
→ The tile they drew does not fit their hand at all
→ Their hand structure has not changed

If a player draws, thinks, and discards a different tile (tedashi):
→ The drawn tile was useful
→ They rearranged their hand
→ The discarded tile was less useful than what they drew
→ Their hand just improved
```

### Dangerous Opponent Signals

Watch for these warning signs:

| Signal | Meaning | Your Response |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Player declares riichi | They are tenpai (100% certain) | Evaluate push/fold |
| Player's discards shift from honors to suited tiles | Likely approaching tenpai | Increase caution |
| Player claims a third meld | Very close to winning (only 1 meld + pair needed) | Consider folding |
| Long pause followed by unusual discard | Player is struggling to find safe tiles | Someone else may be tenpai |
| Player discards a dora tile | They have a strong hand that does not need the dora, or they are folding | Pay attention to context |

### Common Mistakes

- **Not looking at discards at all.** Many beginners focus only on their own hand. Force yourself to look at opponent discard pools every few turns.
- **Over-reading.** Sometimes a discard is just a discard. Do not construct elaborate theories from limited data.
- **Forgetting to count tiles.** Track how many copies of key tiles have appeared. If 3 of the 4 copies of `5m` are visible, the fourth is unlikely to appear.

### Practice Exercise

1. Before each discard, glance at every opponent's discard pool. Note one observation.
2. After an opponent declares riichi, try to identify 3 safe tiles in your hand using their discard pool.
3. When an opponent opens melds, mentally predict what kind of hand they might be building.

## Chapter 12: Strategy Tips

**Learning Objective:** Apply 20 practical tips to improve your game immediately.

### Beginner Tips (Tips 1-7)

**Tip 1: Sort your hand, always.**
Keep tiles organized by suit. It helps you see connections and melds faster. The game has an auto-sort feature — use it.

**Tip 2: Discard honor tiles you do not need early.**
Single wind tiles (that are not your seat wind or round wind) are usually the safest early discards and the least useful to keep.

**Tip 3: Do not claim chows for the first 20 games.**
Seriously. Learning to play with a closed hand teaches you better habits. You will develop patience and learn to appreciate hand value.

**Tip 4: Always check if you are in furiten before hoping for ron.**
Look at your discard pool. Are any of your winning tiles there? If yes, you can only win by tsumo.

**Tip 5: Count to four.**
There are exactly 4 copies of each tile. If you can see 3 copies of a tile you need (in discards, open melds, and your hand), only 1 copy remains.

**Tip 6: Learn the dora indicator reading.**
When the dora indicator is `3p`, the actual dora is `4p`. The "next" tile in sequence is the dora. This adds free han to your hand.

**Tip 7: Riichi is your best friend.**
When in doubt and you are tenpai with a closed hand, declare riichi. It guarantees you have a yaku, gives you a chance at ippatsu and ura dora, and puts pressure on opponents.

### Intermediate Tips (Tips 8-14)

**Tip 8: Think about hand value BEFORE reaching tenpai.**
If your hand is heading toward 1 han with no dora, consider whether you can redirect toward a higher-value hand before committing.

**Tip 9: Two-sided waits win games.**
Shape your hand to end with a ryanmen (two-sided) wait whenever possible. The difference between waiting on 4 tiles and 8 tiles is enormous.

**Tip 10: Keep dora tiles.**
Dora tiles are worth 1 han each. Build your hand around them when possible. A dora in your hand is like having a free yaku.

**Tip 11: Match the round's pace.**
If the round is fast (opponents are claiming tiles and approaching tenpai quickly), speed up your hand. If the round is slow, aim for value.

**Tip 12: Your position matters.**
If you are in last place, take more risks. If you are in first place, play conservatively. Mahjong rewards position-appropriate play.

**Tip 13: Open hands need a plan.**
If you open your hand (claim a tile), know exactly what yaku you are going for. Do not open without a clear path to a valid, valuable win.

**Tip 14: Pay attention to the round wind and your seat wind.**
A pong of your seat wind or the round wind is worth 1 han and can be the yaku that enables your win. Do not discard them carelessly.

### Advanced Tips (Tips 15-20)

**Tip 15: Learn when NOT to declare riichi.**
If you already have 3+ han and a good wait, damaten (hidden tenpai) might be better. You keep flexibility and can still win without the 1,000-point deposit risk.

**Tip 16: The first few discards from each player are information gold.**
A player who discards `1m, 9m, 1p, 9s` in the first round is almost certainly going for tanyao (all simples). Adjust your defense accordingly.

**Tip 17: Count winning tiles, not just wait types.**
A two-sided wait is only good if the tiles are actually still available. A closed wait on a live tile can be better than a two-sided wait on tiles that are mostly gone.

**Tip 18: Betaori (full defense) is a valid strategy.**
Sometimes the best play is to win zero points this round rather than lose 8,000. Do not feel like you must always be attacking.

**Tip 19: Track the wall count.**
The number of remaining wall tiles tells you how much time you have. When the wall is running low, switch to defense if you are not tenpai.

**Tip 20: Review your hands after the game.**
Use the replay system to look at your decisions. Ask yourself: "What tile should I have discarded? When should I have folded?" This is the fastest way to improve.
