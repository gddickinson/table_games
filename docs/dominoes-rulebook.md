## Game Overview

Block Dominoes is one of the oldest and most widely played domino games in the world. It is a game of strategy, tile management, and deduction played by 2 to 4 players. The standard version uses a double-six domino set containing 28 tiles.

### Objective

Be the first player to play all of your tiles, or have the lowest pip count when the game becomes blocked. Points are scored based on the pip counts remaining in opponents' hands.

### Key Characteristics

- **Players:** 2 to 4 (best with 4 in two partnerships)
- **Equipment:** Standard double-six domino set (28 tiles)
- **Type:** Tile-matching strategy game
- **Skill vs. Luck:** Moderate luck in the deal, significant skill in play
- **Average Game Duration:** 10-20 minutes per round
- **Origin:** China (12th century), popularized in Europe in the 18th century

### What Makes Block Dominoes Unique

Unlike "Draw" dominoes, in Block Dominoes there is **no drawing from a boneyard**. If you cannot play a tile from your hand, you must pass. This creates a more strategic game where blocking opponents becomes a central tactic, and every tile played (or not played) provides information about what the other players hold.

### Total Pip Count

The total number of pips across all 28 tiles is **168**. This number is useful for tile counting and score verification.

### The Seven Doubles

The set contains seven **doubles** (tiles with the same value on both ends):

- 0-0 (double blank) — 0 pips
- 1-1 (double one) — 2 pips
- 2-2 (double two) — 4 pips
- 3-3 (double three) — 6 pips
- 4-4 (double four) — 8 pips
- 5-5 (double five) — 10 pips
- 6-6 (double six) — 12 pips

Doubles play a special role in the game and are traditionally placed perpendicular to the line of play.

## Tile Anatomy

### Structure of a Domino Tile

Each domino tile is a rectangular piece divided into two square halves by a center line. Each half contains **pips** (dots) representing a numerical value from 0 to 6.

- **Ends:** The two halves of a tile, each showing a value.
- **Pips:** The dots on each end indicating the value. A blank end has zero pips.
- **Face:** The side of the tile showing the pips.
- **Back:** The blank reverse side, identical for all tiles to prevent identification.
- **Spinner:** A double that can be played on all four sides (in some game variants, not standard Block Dominoes).
- **Center Line:** The dividing line between the two halves.

### Reading a Tile

Tiles are typically described by their two end values, with the higher value first by convention:

- **6-3** (six-three): one end has 6 pips, the other has 3 pips
- **5-5** (double five): both ends have 5 pips
- **4-0** (four-blank): one end has 4 pips, the other is blank
- **0-0** (double blank): both ends are blank

The orientation of a tile in your hand does not matter — a 6-3 is the same tile as a 3-6.

### Pip Counts

The **pip count** of a tile is the total number of pips on both ends:

- 6-5 has a pip count of 11 (the highest non-double)
- 6-6 has a pip count of 12 (the highest overall)
- 0-0 has a pip count of 0 (the lowest)
- The average pip count per tile is 6 (168 total pips / 28 tiles)

## Setup and Dealing

### Shuffling

All 28 tiles are placed **face down** on the table and thoroughly mixed. Players shuffle the tiles by moving them around randomly with their hands. This shuffled pool of face-down tiles is called the **boneyard**.

### Dealing

After shuffling, tiles are distributed to players based on the number of players:

| Players | Tiles Per Player | Tiles in Boneyard |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 2 | 7 each | 14 remaining |
| 3 | 7 each (or 9 each) | 7 remaining (or 1) |
| 4 | 7 each | 0 remaining |

In the standard 4-player game, **all 28 tiles are dealt** and there is no boneyard. Each player has exactly 7 tiles. This is the purest form of Block Dominoes because all tiles are in play and no information is hidden in a draw pile.

### Arranging Your Hand

After receiving your tiles, arrange them face-up in front of you (hidden from other players). Common organization methods include:

- **By value:** Group tiles that share a common end value together.
- **By pip count:** Arrange from highest to lowest pip count.
- **By playability:** Group tiles based on how likely they are to be playable.

Standing tiles on their edges facing you (like a hand of cards) is the traditional way to hold dominoes so opponents cannot see them.

## Determining the First Player

### First Round

The player holding the **highest double** goes first. The search for the first player follows this order:

1. Double 6 (6-6)
2. Double 5 (5-5)
3. Double 4 (4-4)
4. Double 3 (3-3)
5. Double 2 (2-2)
6. Double 1 (1-1)
7. Double blank (0-0)

If no player holds a double (extremely unlikely with 4 players since all 7 doubles are dealt), the player with the highest-pip tile goes first.

The first player **must play their highest double** as the opening tile.

### Subsequent Rounds

In subsequent rounds, the first player is typically:

- The **winner of the previous round**, or
- Play rotates **clockwise** from the previous starting player, or
- The player with the highest double again (varies by house rules).

In this game, the winner of the previous round goes first.

## Gameplay

### Basic Play

Starting with the first player and proceeding **clockwise**, each player takes a turn. On your turn, you must play one tile from your hand onto the layout if you have a legal play. A legal play means one end of your tile **matches** a value at one of the **open ends** of the layout.

### The Layout (Line of Play)

The layout is the arrangement of tiles on the table. It starts as a single tile and grows as players add tiles to either end. The layout always has exactly **two open ends** (except when the first tile is played, which establishes both ends).

**Example progression:**

1. First player plays 6-6. Open ends: 6 and 6.
2. Next player plays 6-3. Open ends: 3 and 6.
3. Next player plays 6-2. Open ends: 3 and 2.
4. Next player plays 3-5. Open ends: 5 and 2.

### Matching Rules

To play a tile, one of its ends must match the value at an open end of the layout:

- If the layout shows a 5 on one end and a 2 on the other, you can play any tile that has a 5 or a 2.
- You place the matching end **adjacent to** the corresponding open end.
- The non-matching end of your tile becomes the new open end.

### Placement

Tiles are placed end-to-end in a line. When the line reaches the edge of the table, it turns at a right angle and continues. The physical layout does not affect gameplay — only the values at the two open ends matter.

### One Tile Per Turn

You must play **exactly one tile per turn** (you cannot play multiple tiles). You must play if you can — passing is only allowed when you have no legal play.

### Choosing Which End to Play On

When you have multiple legal plays, you choose which tile to play and which end of the layout to play it on. This choice is the primary source of strategy in Block Dominoes (see the Strategy Guide for detailed guidance).

## Passing

### When You Must Pass

If you have no tile in your hand that matches either open end of the layout, you **must pass** (also called "knocking"). You do not draw any tiles — in Block Dominoes, there is no drawing from the boneyard during play (in the 4-player version, there is no boneyard at all).

### Announcing a Pass

When passing, you must announce it to the other players. Common ways to indicate a pass:

- Say "Pass"
- Say "Go"
- Knock on the table (the origin of the term "knock")

In this game, the system automatically detects when you have no legal play and passes for you.

### Strategic Implications of Passing

A pass reveals important information to all players:

- You do **not** have any tile matching the current open ends.
- Other players can deduce which values you are missing.
- Opponents can try to keep those values as open ends to force you to continue passing.

This is one of the most important strategic concepts in Block Dominoes — see the Strategy Guide for details on reading opponents' passes.

## Playing Doubles

### Perpendicular Placement

Doubles are traditionally placed **perpendicular** (crosswise) to the line of play, rather than end-to-end like non-doubles. This is purely a visual convention and does not affect gameplay.

### Doubles as Connectors

When a double is in the line of play, the matching value continues through both of its ends since both ends show the same value:

**Example:**
- Layout: 5-3 | 3-3 | 3-6
- The double 3 connects the 5-3 to the 3-6.
- The open ends are 5 and 6 (not 3).

### Playing on a Double

In standard Block Dominoes, doubles are treated identically to non-doubles in terms of play. They have the same value on both ends, so playing on a double simply means matching that value.

Some variants (like Muggins or Mexican Train) allow branching from doubles (playing on all four sides), but this is **not** a rule in standard Block Dominoes.

### The First Double

The first tile played is traditionally a double (the highest double held). It is placed in the center of the playing area and establishes both open ends at the same value.

## Ending a Round

A round ends in one of two ways:

### 1. A Player Goes Out (Domino)

The round ends immediately when a player plays their **last tile**. That player "goes out" or "dominoes" and wins the round. The winning player scores points based on the tiles remaining in all opponents' hands.

### 2. The Game Becomes Blocked

If all players consecutively pass (no one can make a legal play), the game is **blocked**. The round ends and the player with the **lowest total pip count** in their remaining hand wins the round.

In a blocked game:

1. All players reveal their remaining tiles.
2. Each player totals the pip count of their remaining tiles.
3. The player with the lowest total wins.
4. That player scores the total pip count of all opponents' remaining tiles (minus their own, in some scoring variants).

### Ties in a Blocked Game

If two or more players tie for the lowest pip count in a blocked game:

- The player with **fewer tiles remaining** wins.
- If still tied, the player who **last played a tile** wins.
- If still tied, no points are scored and the next round begins with the tied player closest to the dealer's left.

## Scoring

### Points Per Round

The winner of each round earns points equal to the **total pip count of all tiles remaining in opponents' hands**.

**Example scoring (4-player game):**

Player A goes out (plays last tile).

| Player | Remaining Tiles | Pip Count |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Player A (winner) | None | 0 |
| Player B | 3-5, 2-4 | 14 |
| Player C | 6-6, 1-0 | 13 |
| Player D | 4-4, 5-3, 2-1 | 19 |

Player A scores: 14 + 13 + 19 = **46 points**

### Scoring in a Blocked Game

When the game is blocked, the winner (lowest pip count) scores the **total of all opponents' pip counts minus their own remaining pip count**.

**Example blocked game scoring:**

| Player | Remaining Pip Count |
|--------|-------------------|
| Player A | 8 (winner - lowest) |
| Player B | 15 |
| Player C | 22 |
| Player D | 11 |

Player A scores: (15 + 22 + 11) - 8 = **40 points**

Some scoring variants award the winner the total of all opponents' pips without subtracting their own. House rules vary — clarify before playing.

### Running Score

Points accumulate across multiple rounds. The game continues until one player reaches a predetermined target score.

### Target Scores

Common target scores for a complete game:

| Number of Players | Typical Target Score |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| 2 players | 100 points |
| 3 players | 100 points |
| 4 players | 150 points |
| Partnership (2v2) | 200 points |

In this game, the target score is configurable in the settings.

## Winning the Game

The first player (or partnership) to reach the **target score** wins the game. The game may extend beyond the target if the final round pushes multiple players over the target simultaneously — in that case, the player with the highest score wins.

### Winning Strategies (Overview)

- Play your high-pip tiles early to minimize the damage if you lose.
- Track which values have been played to predict opponents' hands.
- Block opponents by playing tiles that leave open ends they cannot match.
- Keep a diverse hand so you always have something to play.
- Watch what opponents pass on to deduce their holdings.

See the complete Strategy Guide for detailed tactics.

## Blocked Games

A blocked game is one of the most strategic and interesting situations in Block Dominoes.

### How a Block Occurs

A game becomes blocked when **no player can make a legal play**. This happens when:

- All tiles showing the values on both open ends have been played.
- The remaining tiles in players' hands do not match either open end.

### Recognizing an Impending Block

You can anticipate a block by tracking tiles:

- If you notice that 7 out of 8 tiles containing a certain value have been played, and the 8th is at an open end, only the player holding a tile with that value can play on that end.
- If both open ends are "depleted" (most tiles of that value already played), a block is likely.

### Causing a Block Intentionally

Advanced players sometimes deliberately cause a block when they have a low pip count relative to opponents. By playing tiles that create open ends that no one (or only you) can match, you can force a block and win on pip count.

### Avoiding a Block

Conversely, if your pip count is high, try to keep the game flowing:

- Play tiles that open up common values.
- Avoid depleting a value completely at an open end.
- Keep tiles that match multiple values in your hand for flexibility.

## Two-Player and Three-Player Variants

### Two-Player Block Dominoes

With 2 players, each player receives **7 tiles** and 14 tiles remain in the boneyard. The boneyard tiles are **not used** during the game — they remain face down and unknown.

This variant adds significant uncertainty because nearly half the tiles are hidden. Strategy shifts toward:

- Less reliable tile counting (you cannot account for boneyard tiles).
- More cautious blocking (the opponent may have tiles you cannot predict).
- Greater emphasis on hand management.

### Three-Player Block Dominoes

With 3 players, each receives **7 tiles** and 7 tiles remain in the boneyard. Alternatively, each player may receive **9 tiles** with 1 tile left in the boneyard.

The 7-tile variant creates moderate uncertainty (7 unknown tiles), while the 9-tile variant is nearly full-information (only 1 unknown tile).

### Draw Dominoes (Popular Variant)

While not the focus of this rulebook, Draw Dominoes is the most common casual variant:

- Players draw from the boneyard when they cannot play.
- Players continue drawing until they get a playable tile or the boneyard is exhausted.
- If the boneyard is empty and you cannot play, you pass.

Draw Dominoes has less strategic depth than Block Dominoes because the boneyard acts as a safety net. Block Dominoes rewards better hand management and tile counting.

## Other Popular Domino Games

While this game features Block Dominoes, here is a brief overview of other popular domino games for reference:

### Muggins (All Fives)

- Score points during play by making the open ends sum to a multiple of 5.
- The first tile played sets one scoring end; each subsequent play can score.
- More dynamic scoring than Block Dominoes.

### Mexican Train

- Each player has their own "train" (line of tiles) plus a communal "Mexican train."
- Players can play on their own train, the Mexican train, or other players' trains under certain conditions.
- More accessible and social than traditional Block Dominoes.

### Chickenfoot

- When a double is played, the next three plays must be on that double (forming a "chickenfoot" shape).
- Play is blocked on other ends until the chickenfoot is completed.
- Adds an interesting tactical element.

### Matador

- Instead of matching end values, the sum of the played end and the open end must equal 7.
- Blanks can only be played with a "matador" (0-0, 1-6, 2-5, 3-4).
- A fundamentally different strategic experience.

### Bergen

- Score points for making both open ends the same value ("double header") or matching a double ("triple header").
- Encourages setting up matching ends.

## Etiquette and Table Rules

### General Etiquette

- **Keep tiles hidden.** Do not let other players see your tiles. Stand them on their edges facing you.
- **Shuffle thoroughly.** When it is your turn to shuffle, move the tiles around face-down for at least 15-20 seconds.
- **Announce passes clearly.** Do not stay silent when you cannot play — knock or say "pass."
- **Play at a reasonable pace.** While strategy requires thought, excessively slow play disrupts the game flow.
- **Do not react to your draw.** Avoid visible reactions when you see your tiles, as this gives information to opponents.
- **No table talk.** Do not hint at or discuss the tiles in your hand.

### Handling Disputes

- **Exposed tile during shuffle:** If a tile flips face-up during dealing, reshuffle.
- **Illegal play:** If a player places a tile that does not match an open end, the tile must be taken back and a legal play made instead. If noticed after the next player has already played, the illegal tile stays (in casual play).
- **Wrong number of tiles dealt:** If discovered before the first play, redeal. If discovered during play, continue playing (the short-handed player has a disadvantage).

### In This Game

- The game enforces all rules automatically.
- Illegal plays are not possible — only legal tiles can be selected.
- Passing is handled automatically when no play is available.
- Scoring is calculated automatically at the end of each round.
